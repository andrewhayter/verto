**Bottom line:** Car insurance premiums are actuarially calculated from eight key risk factors. Driving record and credit score carry the most weight. Location is largely fixed, but knowing which factors apply to you lets you optimize the ones you can influence — and understand why you're paying what you're paying.

---

Car insurance underwriting is not guesswork. Actuaries analyze decades of claims data to identify which characteristics predict claim frequency and severity. Your premium is the output of that calculation applied to your specific risk profile.

Here are the eight factors that actually determine your rate — and how much each one matters.

## Factor 1: Driving Record (Weight: Very High)

Your claims and violation history is the most predictive individual factor. Typical surcharges at renewal:

- **At-fault accident:** +40–60% for 3–5 years
- **Speeding ticket (1–14 mph over):** +20–25% for 3 years
- **Speeding ticket (15–29 mph over):** +30–45% for 3–5 years
- **DUI/DWI:** +70–100%+ for 5–10 years, may trigger SR-22 requirement
- **Reckless driving:** +80–100%+ for 5 years

A clean record is the best discount available. Defensive driving courses can reduce the impact of a ticket in some states — check with your insurer before paying for one.

## Factor 2: Location (Weight: Very High)

Your ZIP code feeds directly into actuarial tables for:
- Theft and vandalism frequency
- Traffic density and accident likelihood
- Weather event exposure
- State tort environment (litigation costs)
- Local repair labor rates

Moving to a suburb vs. a dense city can reduce premiums 30–50%. Within cities, ZIP code matters — some urban neighborhoods run 40% higher than adjacent ones due to parking and theft statistics.

## Factor 3: Credit-Based Insurance Score (Weight: High, Varies by State)

Forty-six states allow insurers to use credit-based insurance scores. This is distinct from your regular credit score but correlated with it.

The actuarial basis: insurers found that credit score predicts claim frequency at the portfolio level. Regulatory debates continue, but the pricing practice is legal in most states and heavily used by major carriers.

**Premium impact by credit tier (same driver, same coverage):**
- Excellent credit vs. poor credit: 50–80% premium difference
- Poor credit vs. average credit: 25–40% difference

Improving your credit score improves your insurance rate at renewal in states where this factor is used.

## Factor 4: Vehicle (Weight: Moderate-High)

Insurers rate each vehicle make, model, and year based on:

**Repair cost:** High-tech vehicles with sensors in bumpers, cameras in mirrors, and electronics in door panels cost significantly more to repair after even minor collisions. A 2024 vehicle with ADAS systems costs more to insure than an older vehicle with similar market value.

**Theft rate:** The NICB publishes annual most-stolen vehicle lists. Chevrolet and Ford full-size pickups, Honda CR-Vs, and Chevrolet Equinoxes appear consistently. If your vehicle is on a frequent-theft list, your comprehensive rate reflects it.

**Safety ratings:** Higher IIHS and NHTSA safety ratings reduce bodily injury claim expectations and can reduce liability and medical payments coverage costs.

**Horsepower/class:** Sports cars and performance vehicles carry higher rates due to actuarial data on driving behavior correlations.

## Factor 5: Age and Gender (Weight: Moderate)

Actuarial data shows:

- **Drivers under 25** pay 1.5–2.5x more than equivalent 35-year-old drivers — claim frequency for young drivers is significantly higher
- **Teen drivers** on a policy raise family premiums substantially (50–100% depending on insurer)
- **Gender:** Males under 25 pay more than females in the same age group in most states (California and a few others have banned gender rating)
- **Seniors (70+):** Some increase in rates due to accident frequency data, though less severe than young driver surcharges

## Factor 6: Annual Mileage (Weight: Moderate)

More miles driven = more exposure to risk. Insurers assess:
- Miles driven annually (self-reported or telematics-measured)
- Primary use (personal, commuting, rideshare)
- Daily commute distance

Low-mileage discounts: typically apply under 7,500 miles/year. If you work remotely and drive significantly below average, inform your insurer — you may qualify for discounts you're not receiving.

## Factor 7: Coverage Levels and Deductibles (Weight: Direct)

Your choices directly affect your premium:

- **Liability limits:** State minimums are usually insufficient. Going from 25/50/25 to 100/300/100 adds $100–$200/year but dramatically changes your exposure in a serious accident.
- **Collision deductible:** Raising from $500 to $1,000 typically saves $100–$200/year. If you could comfortably cover $1,000 out of pocket, the higher deductible is mathematically favorable over 3–5 years.
- **Comprehensive:** Usually cheap ($100–$200/year) and worth keeping unless the vehicle is very low value.
- **Add-ons:** Rental reimbursement, roadside assistance, gap insurance — each adds cost, each may be duplicated elsewhere.

### What factors affect car insurance rates most?

Driving record and location are the two biggest factors. A single at-fault accident raises premiums 40–60% for 3–5 years. Your ZIP code can change your premium by 20–40% if you cross into a high-theft or high-accident area. Credit score affects premiums by up to 80% in 46 states. Getting three competing quotes for the same coverage routinely reveals a $600+/year gap between insurers.

## Factor 8: Insurer-Specific Pricing (Weight: Very High for Comparison)

The same driver profile — same record, same vehicle, same location, same coverage — receives meaningfully different quotes from different insurers. Pricing algorithms, portfolio mix, target customer profiles, and competitive strategy all vary.

This is why comparison shopping is the highest-leverage tactic for most drivers. The spread between the most and least expensive quote for the same coverage regularly exceeds $600/year.
