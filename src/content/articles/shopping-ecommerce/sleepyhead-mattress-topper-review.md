## The Problem: A 7-Year-Old Mattress That Wasn't Bad Enough to Replace

My mattress situation is probably familiar: a mid-range queen that cost $900 seven years ago, still structurally sound but progressively less comfortable. No visible sagging. Just firmer than it used to be, with a tendency to sleep warmer than it should.

I wasn't sleeping badly — I was sleeping adequately but not well. The Oura Ring data was showing: average 48 minutes of deep sleep per night (recommended 1–2 hours), 4.2 restless periods per night, and an efficiency score (time asleep vs. time in bed) of about 81%.

The fork: buy a new mattress ($1,200–$2,500 for a quality queen) or try a topper first.

### H3: Do mattress toppers improve deep sleep?

Mattress toppers can increase deep sleep duration if the current sleep surface is causing discomfort, pressure points, or heat that disrupts sleep continuity. A cooling gel topper can reduce surface temperature by 2–5°F — significant for hot sleepers since research shows a 2–4°F surface cooling increase deep sleep duration. Pressure relief reduces micro-arousals from pressure point discomfort, which appear as restless periods in sleep tracking data.

## What Didn't Change

Sleep onset time: similar throughout. The topper addresses comfort during sleep, not the ability to fall asleep initially — that's a different category of sleep issue.

Total sleep duration: I wasn't sleeping more hours, just better hours.

If my sleep problems were from other causes (caffeine, late-night phone use, stress, sleep apnea), the topper wouldn't have moved these metrics. The specific improvements I saw are consistent with a comfort-based intervention.
