## How Loan-Matching Platforms Work

A loan-matching platform is not a lender—it is a single application that submits your profile to a network of multiple lenders simultaneously.

You fill out one form (typically 2–4 minutes). The platform runs a soft credit inquiry—which does not affect your credit score—and returns competing loan offers from lenders that have pre-approved your profile. You review the offers, select the best terms, and proceed only with the lender you choose.

A hard credit inquiry only occurs when you formally apply with a specific lender after selecting their offer. Until that point, your score is unaffected regardless of how many platforms you use.

## Five Things That Affect Your Personal Loan Rate

Understanding these factors before you apply allows you to position your application for the best possible terms:

1. **Credit score.** The single largest rate driver. Each 50-point improvement can reduce APR by 3–6 percentage points.
2. **Debt-to-income ratio (DTI).** Most lenders prefer DTI below 43%. Calculate yours: monthly debt payments ÷ gross monthly income.
3. **Loan amount relative to income.** Requesting a smaller amount relative to your monthly income signals lower risk to lenders.
4. **Loan term.** Shorter terms carry lower rates but higher monthly payments. A 24-month term almost always carries a lower rate than a 60-month term.
5. **Loan purpose.** Debt consolidation is the most favorably viewed purpose by most lenders—it reduces overall DTI if you close the accounts being consolidated.

## Debt Consolidation: When a Personal Loan Makes Sense

A personal loan for debt consolidation makes financial sense when your new loan's rate is meaningfully lower than the weighted average of your current balances. The math:

- Average credit card APR in 2026: 20.1% (Federal Reserve)
- Personal loan rate for good credit: 12%–18%
- Potential interest savings on $15,000 consolidated balance over 3 years: $2,100–$4,800

Consolidation also reduces the number of minimum payments, which reduces missed payment risk.

**When it does not make sense:** If your credit score does not produce a rate materially lower than your existing balances, or if you would extend the loan term significantly (a 36-month credit card payoff on a 60-month loan increases total interest paid even at a lower rate).
