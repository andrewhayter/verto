## Why Men's Hormones Change After 35—And What That Means Clinically

Men's health declines after 35 are not random. They follow documented biological timelines:

**Growth hormone:** Production peaks in the mid-20s and declines 14% per decade. By 50, most men produce roughly half the growth hormone they did at 25. The result: slower muscle recovery, increased fat accumulation, reduced sleep quality, and declining mental sharpness.

**NAD+:** This coenzyme is required for mitochondrial energy production and DNA repair. Research published in *Cell Metabolism* (2020) documented a 50% drop in NAD+ levels between ages 40 and 60. Low NAD+ correlates with fatigue, brain fog, and impaired cellular recovery.

**DHT and hair follicles:** Dihydrotestosterone (DHT) is produced when testosterone converts via the 5-alpha reductase enzyme. In men with androgenetic alopecia, DHT binds to hair follicle receptors, shrinking the follicle until it stops producing visible hair. DHT-blocking medications work by preventing this conversion.

**Erectile function:** ED affects 52% of men between 40 and 70, per the Massachusetts Male Aging Study. Vascular, hormonal, and psychological factors are all contributors—telehealth prescriptions typically address the vascular and hormonal components.
