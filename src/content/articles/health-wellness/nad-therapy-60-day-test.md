## The Problem Nobody Warns You About Before Your Mid-40s

I'm 44. I run five days a week, sleep 7.5 hours, don't drink, and eat reasonably well. Two years ago I started ending workday meetings at 2pm with concentration I couldn't fake. By 3pm I was running on fumes. My bloodwork came back clean. My doctor said "it's probably stress."

It wasn't stress. It was a coenzyme.

NAD+ (nicotinamide adenine dinucleotide) is required for every one of your cells to produce energy. It sits at the center of mitochondrial function — the cellular process that converts food into ATP, the molecule your body actually runs on. Without adequate NAD+, mitochondria operate at reduced efficiency. The result is fatigue that no amount of sleep or coffee fixes, because the problem is upstream of both.

Here's what I didn't know: NAD+ levels decline roughly 1–2% per year from your 30s onward. By your mid-40s, you have about 50% of the NAD+ you had at 20. That's not a marginal reduction. That's half the fuel in your cellular engine.

## What the Research Actually Shows

A 2022 meta-analysis in *Cell Metabolism* confirmed that NAD+ precursor supplementation increases cellular NAD+ levels in humans — that part is settled. The debate is around which outcomes follow.

The strongest evidence is for:

**Metabolic function:** A 2023 randomized controlled trial in *Nature Aging* found NMN supplementation improved insulin sensitivity and muscle function in adults over 65. An earlier 2021 trial found improvements in aerobic capacity at 250mg NMN daily in recreational runners.

**DNA repair:** NAD+ is consumed by PARP enzymes (which repair DNA strand breaks) and sirtuins (which regulate gene expression in response to stress). When NAD+ is depleted, these repair processes slow — which is why NAD+ decline is mechanistically linked to accelerated cellular aging, not just correlated with it.

**Sleep architecture:** NAD+ is required for NAMPT, the rate-limiting enzyme in the circadian clock. Low NAD+ disrupts sleep quality independent of sleep duration. This is why NAD+ depletion can produce fatigue even when total sleep hours are adequate.

The weaker evidence — and I'll be direct — is for acute cognitive sharpening. Several companies claim NAD+ produces rapid mental clarity improvements. The mechanism doesn't support rapid onset; NAD+ is a cellular substrate, not a stimulant. Don't expect a nootropic effect in week one.
